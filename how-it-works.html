🎲 How "Chance to Win the Pot" Works

The maths behind the Monte Carlo simulation — explained with real numbers from our game.

1 · The game in one paragraph

10 players paid ฿1,000 each (pot ฿10,000). Before the tournament, everyone drafted 7 national teams. Teams earn points by how far they go: champion 120 · runner-up 90 · 3rd 65 · 4th 50 · out at QF 30 · out at R16 15 · out at R32 5 · out in groups −40. Highest total takes 75% of the pot, 2nd takes 20%, and 3rd-from-last gets a 5% consolation. While a team is still alive it counts its guaranteed floor (already in the QF = at least 30, etc.).

2 · Why simulate whole brackets, not teams?

A naive approach draws each team's final position independently: "France champion with 29%, Argentina champion with 16%…" — draw a random finish for each team, add up points. The problem: one random draw can produce three champions, or six semi-finalists. Real tournaments have exactly one champion, one runner-up, four QF losers, eight R16 losers. And two teams in the same half of the draw can never meet in the final — independent draws don't know that.

So instead, every simulation run plays the actual knockout tree once, match by match:

M97 (QF1) = W(M89 Paraguay–France) v W(M90 Canada–Morocco) ┐ M98 (QF2) = W(M93 Portugal–Spain) v W(M94 USA–Belgium) ┴→ M101 (SF1) ┐ M99 (QF3) = W(M91 Brazil–Norway) v W(M92 Mexico–England) ┐ ├→ M104 FINAL M100(QF4) = W(M95 Argentina–Egypt) v W(M96 Swi.–Colombia) ┴→ M102 (SF2) ┘ SF losers → M103 (3rd place)

Each run therefore produces one coherent tournament — exactly one champion, correct counts at every stage, and bracket collisions respected. Repeat 10,000 times, and count how often each player finishes 1st, 2nd, or 3rd-from-last. Those frequencies are the percentages on the scoreboard.

3 · Where do the match odds come from?

Opta (The Analyst) publishes each team's probability of reaching each stage. We convert those into per-match win chances using conditional probability:

pWin(round) = reach(next stage) ÷ reach(current stage)
P(A beats B) = pA ÷ (pA + pB)

In the R16 this is easy — every survivor's reach-R16 is 100%, so pWin is just the reach-QF number (Morocco 67.0% v Canada 33.0%, and they sum to 100 because Opta knows the pairing). The interesting case is a later round, where the reach numbers bundle up everything that must happen first:

Worked example — a France v Morocco quarter-final (M97): Opta says France reaches the QF with 86.62% and the SF with 67.39%. That 67.39% is the chance of two things together: getting to the QF and winning it. To get the pure "win the QF" chance we divide out the part that's already happened:

France: pWin(QF) = 67.39 ÷ 86.62 = 0.778 ← "given France is IN the QF, it wins 77.8% of them"
Morocco: pWin(QF) = 20.72 ÷ 67.01 = 0.309
P(France beats Morocco) = 0.778 ÷ (0.778 + 0.309) = 71.6%

Note the two ratios don't sum to 1 (0.778 + 0.309 = 1.087) — because Opta's averages include all possible QF opponents, not just each other. The normalisation step fixes that for the specific pairing. This is also the one approximation in the engine: a team's pWin is its average over likely opponents, so a favourite meeting a weak team is slightly underrated (see §6).

Already-decided games are never re-simulated. Once ESPN reports a result, that match is locked in every run — after Morocco beat Canada, all 10,000 runs start from that fact. This is also why the odds stay valid even between Opta updates: the ratios are conditional probabilities, so locking in results updates the maths automatically (Bayes' rule doing its job quietly).

5 · Why the pot odds aren't just "who has the best teams"

Two things make this game interesting beyond raw team strength. Shared picks cancel out: seven players drafted Morocco, so Morocco winning lifts almost everyone equally — but a team only one player holds (like Dha's France) moves that player alone. The bracket creates collisions: Portugal and Spain meet in the R16, so Kirk/Vor/Win's Portugal and Yo/Jill/Jew's Spain can't both go deep — every run knocks one side out early. The simulation prices all of this automatically, because it plays real tournaments.

6 · Trust, but verify — the calibration check

Next to each team's Opta champion-% the scoreboard shows a small grey number: the simulation's own champion rate. If the bracket assumption were wrong, these would diverge sharply (that's exactly how we caught a bad pairing once). A small gap remains by design: our engine uses each team's average advance rate, while Opta's model knows the specific likely opponent each round — so favourites read a few points low (France ~23% vs 28.9%) and long shots slightly high. Mid-strength teams sit right on top of Opta. This residual barely affects the player odds, which is what we actually care about.
